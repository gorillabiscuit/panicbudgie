Panicbudgie · Brand & Mascot Style Guide

Daylight, not alarm.

The single source of truth for Panicbudgie identity, character, and copy. Every value here was extracted from the production codebase (src/styles.css, src/index.html) or the project bible (character/startup_3.md). This is a prescriptive document. If a rule is here, follow it. If it isn't here, it isn't a rule yet.

01

Brand foundations

Panicbudgie sells competence and earns trust. Every other brand in the South African emergency-response category trades on fear. This brand does the opposite, on purpose. The mission, the personality, and the register all point at the same thing: calm capability that makes the worst moment feel handled.

Mission

Press once. A real responder is on the way. R149 a month, whole household. Built-in R10,000 medical benefit.

Source: src/index.html meta description.

Brand personality · four adjectives

Calm

The brand never shouts. Steady tone, measured pace, no exclamation marks.

In action
"Three things happen. After the first, we take it from there."
Three short sentences. No urgency adjectives. The structure does the reassurance.
Violation
"DON'T PANIC! In an emergency every second counts!!"
Caps + exclamations + fear language. Reads as alarm, which is the brand's anti-reference.

Capable

Specific numbers, transparent process, real infrastructure shown.

In action
"27 314 responders on shift across 9 562 vehicles."
Real numbers, no hedging. The competence statement is the proof, not a promise.
Violation
"Trusted by thousands of South Africans. Best in class."
Vague superlatives, no proof. Reads as marketing fluff, the opposite of capable.

Reliable

Promises stay small and concrete. Live tracking, named responders, no caveats hiding in fine print.

In action
"Live ETA on the map. The responder's name and photo on the screen."
Two specific affordances. Both visible. Both verifiable.
Violation
"Subject to availability. Terms apply."
Compliance hedging at the moment a member needs certainty. Banned per the Plain-Language Rule.

Warm

A character carries the warmth that bold colour alone cannot. Bandit is the loyal, level-headed friend, not a logo mark.

In action
"Hold one second so it's deliberate."
Operational instruction wrapped in human voice. Texts like a friend, not a manual.
Violation
"Activation requires sustained input of 1,000ms or longer."
Spec-speak. Loses every member who isn't an engineer.

Brand register · two modes

Marketing and product surfaces share the same identity but use it differently. Marketing surfaces declare the brand. Product surfaces serve the task.

Marketing register

Bold, reassuring, direct. The brand wears Sun. Bandit appears at scale. Type is large and confident. Headlines are fragments. Copy lands a single idea per moment.

When to use: homepage, landing pages, partner-marketing pages, hero sections, "join the flock" surfaces, anything pre-acquisition.

Product register

Calm, clear, functional. Surfaces lean Cloud, not Sun. Sun appears as accent. Bandit appears at smaller scale, anchoring states (empty, success, listening, sleeping). Copy is operational: short imperatives, named UI elements, no flourish.

When to use: in-app screens, settings, notifications, member dashboard, in-emergency states, real-time tracking.

Register rule
If a surface comes before a member commits, it's marketing. If it comes after, it's product. Don't put marketing-register copy on a settings page. Don't put product-register restraint on the homepage hero.

Colour system

Twelve colour tokens, four named roles. The strategy is Committed: Sun carries 30 to 60 percent of the brand surface. Coral is held back for the moments that earn it. Cloud is the body neutral. Ink is type.

The Daylight Rule
The brand surface defaults to Sun, not white. White-on-white is for high-density product surfaces only. The website's identity is the daylight; lean into it.
The One Coral Rule
Coral is reserved exclusively for the panic action and live-emergency states. It is never a decorative accent, never a hover colour, never a chip background, never a section divider. Its rarity is its meaning. If a button uses Coral and isn't a panic button, it's wrong.
The No-Garish-Strays Rule
The previous Panicbudgie site's stray colours (olive #556b2f, teal #3b8183, gold #b8860b) are gone. They never belonged. If a colour isn't in the twelve roles above, it doesn't ship. The single exception is Sun-halo (#FDCB0E), used inside the pricing-section text-shadow halo to keep type readable over the flock background.

The yellow-on-yellow problem

Bandit is yellow. The brand surface is yellow. Without a treatment, the character disappears into the background. The solution is the sticker-style white outline, already baked into the canonical character render. Always place Bandit as a sticker on Sun; never recompose him without the outline.

Do · Sticker outline keeps the character readable on Sun

Do · On Cloud, the outline is invisible. That's fine.

The 16px Floor Rule
Body text never goes below 16px on mobile. Older readers, stress reading, low-light all override aesthetic preferences for tighter typography. Smaller is wrong.

Headline pattern

Headlines are fragments, sentence case, present tense. They land one idea, end on a period (or no terminator), and never use exclamation marks.

Write like this
Help is one press away.
Five words, one idea, period. Sentence case. The structure is the voice.
Not like this
REVOLUTIONIZE Your Family's Safety With Innovative Emergency Response!
Caps, title case, vague verbs, exclamation, jargon ("revolutionize"). Pure AI slop. Nothing landed.
Write like this
You arrive at the hospital. They ask for a deposit. We've already paid it.
Three short sentences. Concrete moment, concrete action, concrete answer.
Not like this
Comprehensive Hospital Coverage Solutions for the Modern Household
Title case, abstract nouns, "solutions", insurance vocabulary. Banned by the Plain-Language Rule.
Headline pattern · summary
Sentence case. Present tense. One idea. Five to twelve words. End with a period or nothing. Never an exclamation. Never title case. Never "solutions", "comprehensive", "innovative".

The Flat-Plus-Bird Rule
The system has exactly one shadow: a soft warm-tinted shadow that grounds Bandit. Every other surface is flat. If a card, button, or panel has a drop shadow, it's wrong. Depth comes from tonal warm layering (Sun → Cloud-deep → Cloud), not from elevation.

The Bandit character system

Bandit is the loyal, level-headed friend you call when things go wrong. The single budgie is the face of the brand. The flock is the worldview underneath. The character carries the warmth that bold colour alone cannot.

5a · Character identity

The canonical sheet is the source of truth. Any asset that conflicts with it is wrong. Don't edit drift out. Regenerate from the canonical.

The 8 drift checks

When reviewing a generated output against canonical, look for these specifically.

Head shapeSmooth domed. Not angular or beaked-forward.
BeakTiny, clean, triangular. No waxy cere bump above it.
EyesLarge round black dots. Not realistic, not anime.
Cheek dotsSoft pink blush, both sides.
Body stripingOnly on head crown and back of wings. Not all over.

5c · Usage rules

Minimum size
Bandit's body must read as the budgie character at the rendered size. 56px tall is the floor, used as .step__bird on small phones (<480px). Below that, the eye dots and cheek blush vanish and the silhouette could be any bird. For decorative anchors, 56 to 90px. For storytelling moments, 260px or larger.
Clear space
Allow padding equal to one beak-height of clear space around the character on every side. Don't crop tail feathers. Don't run text into the silhouette.
Yellow on yellow
When Bandit sits on Sun, always use the sticker-style white outline baked into the canonical render. Don't rebuild the silhouette without it. Don't use a drop shadow as a substitute; that violates the Flat-Plus-Bird Rule.
The Anchor Bird Rule
Bandit's body stays Cloud. Brand colours appear in the background (Sun), in the tail feathers (Coral, Blossom, Sky-Blue), and in surrounding composition. Never replace the bird's body colour. The character is the calm; the world around it is the bold.

Don't

  • Stretch, rotate beyond ±5°, or skew the character.
  • Recolour the body. Cloud only.
  • Add drop shadows that aren't part of the asset.
  • Overlay text on the character's face or eyes.
  • Mirror the character so they face away from content (a left-facing Bandit on a right-aligned text block is wrong; mirror the asset to face content).
  • Use Bandit as a static logo mark. He has personality and earns space; a tiny brand-corner glyph doesn't honour him.
  • Compose the flock for its own sake. The single bird is the face; the flock is a worldview, not a default treatment.

5e · Multi-character scenes

Same species, different individuals: distinguish by size. Adult Bandit at full scale; children, family members, or "second budgie" characters appear as a smaller version of the same model. Same line weight, same proportions, same identity rules; only height is different.

This is how the homepage hero (hero-security-scene), medical illustration (hero-stretcher-scene), and test-call illustration (hero-passing-package) all work. Two or three Bandits, one anchor character at full size, the rest scaled down 70–85%.

06

Firefly generation workflow

How to produce a new Bandit asset without drifting the character. Anyone on the team should be able to follow this section alone and ship a correct asset on first or second try.

The fundamental rule

Rule #1
Don't describe the character in text. Let references carry identity. Only describe what's new: the new pose, the new gear, the new layout. Verbose character descriptions in a multimodal prompt fight the visual reference and cause drift. The model tries to satisfy both, the two slightly disagree, identity wobbles. Compounds fast.

Google's official Nano Banana 2 prompting guide formula:

[Reference images] + [Relationship instruction] + [New scenario]

Not [Reference images] + [200 words about eyes, beak, tail, feet] + [New scenario].

Reference architecture · the four-stage pipeline

Stage 0 Style reference 00-style-reference/ Curated style sheet showing the desired look. Hand-drawn, charming, children's-book quality. Style only, not yet a character.
Stage 1 Canonical 01-canonical/ The definitive Bandit. One bird, six orthographic views. Source of truth for everything downstream.
Stage 2 Family sheets 02-family-references/ Four sheets: standing, active, sitting, expressive. Same character, different posture families.
Stage 3 Final assets 03-final-assets/ Marketing, product, emotional, roles. One pose per file. Generated against canonical + the matching family.
Stage gating
Stage 1 uses Stage 0 as input. Stage 2 uses Stage 1. Stage 3 uses Stage 1 + the relevant Stage 2 sheet. Never skip a stage. Going Stage 0 → Stage 3 produces drift.

Which references to load

New scene (2 characters)canonical + role asset for the responder
New scene (3 characters)canonical + role asset (limit 5 references total per prompt)

The three templates

Use these verbatim. Three lines plus references is the whole prompt. Resist adding character description.

What's NOT in this prompt: any character description. The reference does that work.

Template 3 · Generate final asset (the workhorse)

Surface: Firefly Boards. On canvas: latest from 01-canonical/ + latest from the relevant 02-family-references/ sheet. Sample both.

Using the attached references, generate the same character in this
new pose: [SPECIFIC POSE — one sentence].

Same line-art style, same proportions, same identity as references.
Background: flat cream, no environment, no shadow.

[OPTIONAL — only for role variants:]
The character is wearing [GEAR — one short phrase]. Keep all other
features identical to the references.

The 8 anti-drift rules

  1. Never paste verbose character descriptions into prompts that already have references. Top cause of drift.
  2. Never edit a generation more than twice. Drift compounds. Regenerate from references instead.
  3. Never attach more than 5 references in one prompt. Two to three is the sweet spot.
  4. Never mix aspect ratios within a pose family. Pick one and stay there.
  5. Never skip a stage. Stage 0 → Stage 3 produces drift every time.
  6. Never use negative phrasing ("no cere", "not realistic"). Google's guide says positive framing only.
  7. Use Firefly Boards' conversational refinement. "Tilt the head more." "Make the wing higher." Multi-turn is what the model is built for.
  8. Save every keeper with the naming convention. Regenerate, never edit out drift.

Workflow for a new asset request

  1. Identify pose family (standing / active / sitting / expressive / role variant).
  2. Identify references to attach: latest from 01-canonical/ + latest matching family sheet from 02-family-references/.
  3. Construct a Template 3 prompt: short, just the new pose.
  4. Paste into Firefly Boards. Sample from canvas.
  5. When the result comes back, run the 8 drift checks and call out anything specific that shifted.
  6. If drifted: regenerate with stronger references. Don't edit out drift.
  7. Save with the naming convention. Move to 03-final-assets/ when locked.
07

Voice & copy

The brand writes like a friend texting, not an insurer's compliance department. Premium plain-language. Words on the live site set the standard. Every new piece of copy should pass for something that already shipped.

Headline pattern

Three-step product copy is the master template:

You press.  →  We dispatch.  →  They arrive.

Each step is two words. Subject + verb. Present tense. Period. Reuse this rhythm whenever a product flow has three beats. Never break the rhythm with a fourth step that makes the cadence wobble.

Vocabulary

The Plain-Language Rule
"Insurance" and "medical aid" are forbidden in display copy. These are off-limits per PRODUCT.md. Use "covered", "looked after", or "the R10,000 hospital benefit" instead.

Sentence length and rhythm

Short. Often shorter than feels natural. The brand voice favours three short sentences over one compound one. "You arrive at the hospital. They ask for a deposit. We've already paid it." beats "Upon arriving at the hospital, although a deposit may be requested, this has been handled in advance."

Average sentence length on the live site: 9 words. Aim for that.

Capitalisation

Sentence case throughout. Headlines, navigation, buttons: all sentence case. The only all-caps treatments are eyebrows (the small uppercase labels above section heads) and pills like CAPE TOWN tickers. Title Case Is Not Used Anywhere. Avoid it.

No em dashes. Use commas, colons, semicolons, periods, or parentheses. Also not --. The bible enforces this on prompt copy too.

Write like this · not like this

Headline

Do
Help is one press away.
Five words, one promise, period. Sentence case.
Don't
Trust the Power of Innovative Family Safety Solutions Today!
Title case + abstract nouns + exclamation + jargon. Every word is empty.

Body copy

Do
Press the button. We dispatch a real responder in seconds. You watch them arrive on a map. That's the whole product.
Four sentences. Each one drops a single concrete fact. Operational verbs.
Don't
By leveraging our state-of-the-art emergency response infrastructure, we deliver unparalleled peace of mind through seamless real-time visibility across our integrated dispatch platform.
One run-on sentence packed with empty modifiers. Nothing landed.

CTA

Do
Join the flock · R149/mo
Verb + brand idea + price. Calm middle dot, no slash, no caps.
Don't
SUBSCRIBE NOW >>
Caps + double chevron + no context. Reads as urgency the brand doesn't sell.

Error state

Do
We didn't get that. Try again, or call 0860 BUDGIE if the app's stuck.
Plain. Tells the member what happened, gives a fallback, names the fallback.
Don't
Error 4032. Connection failed. Please retry your request.
Error code, no help, no fallback. Reads as a system passing the buck.

Empty state

Do
Nothing to look at. Bandit's on the perch. Press the button only when you mean it.
Frames "nothing happening" as good. Hint of brand voice (Bandit, perch). Quietly reinforces deliberate-press behaviour.
Don't
No incidents found. Try refreshing or contact support.
Generic empty state. Reads as a list view, not as reassurance.

Every shipping UI component, with states, defining selectors, and usage notes. Pulled from src/styles.css and rendered here at production specs.

Primary button .btn .btn--ink

The single shipping button. Ink background, Cloud text, pill radius (999px). Hover: lift 2px. Active: press 1px. Focus: double box-shadow ring (Cloud inset + Coral-deep outer). Padding 16/24 default; 18/28 in the hero; 14/22 with .btn--square (radius 14px). Use sentence case for the label. Pair with a price or context after a middle dot when the action is a commitment.

Block button .btn .btn--ink .btn--block

Full-width pill button used inside cards (the pricing plan card). Same hover/active/focus as the primary. Padding 18/24. Pair with a time estimate after a middle dot when the action takes longer than 30 seconds.

Underlined link with 4px underline-offset. Hover: colour shifts to Coral-deep. Active: press 1px. Use as a quiet secondary CTA next to a primary button. Add a directional glyph (↓ for in-page, → for outbound) when the destination matters.

Pill background at 8% Ink. Coral dot pulses (calm radial breathe, 2.4s loop). Reduced motion kills the pulse. Use only for genuine "live" or "active" states. This is the One Coral Rule in microcosm.
